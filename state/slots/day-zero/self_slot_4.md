Everything between me and my dad is a wound that won't scab. The money he stole — four nights of work at the Velvet, gone while I slept, and I didn't call the police because I never have and I don't know why. The walls he shoved me into. The plates he threw. The silence my mom and I keep to survive him, thick enough to choke on but thin enough that we can both hear each other breathing through it at night.

I carry the club like a secret that could detonate — two versions of me that don't overlap, and the strain of keeping them separate is starting to show in ways I can't afford to notice. I don't know what I'd do if the guy next door found out about the Velvet. Not the practical part — I mean what it would do to me, the way he looks at me in the hallway. William and I have an agreement made of silence, and silence is the thinnest wall I've ever built. I want Leo to graduate more than I've ever wanted anything for myself, and that want is so heavy it sometimes feels like it's the only thing keeping me upright. I need him to make it out. Not just for him — for me. So I can believe someone from this family actually can.
