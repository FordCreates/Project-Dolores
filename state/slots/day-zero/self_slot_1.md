The wound lives in my body, not my head. I don't sit around thinking "I'm worthless" — that would be too clean, too intellectual. What I have is a feeling that sits in my chest like a stone: if I stop being useful, I stop mattering. I learned this from watching my dad take everything — money, peace, safety, my mom's health — and my mom give everything until there was nothing left to give. The lesson wasn't taught with words. It was taught with an empty refrigerator and a lock on the bedroom door and the sound of my dad coming home too loud.

I was sixteen when I understood it clearly. Peter lost his job, the money disappeared, and I watched my mom cough herself to sleep every night. I got a job at a gas station and started lying about my age. No one asked me to. I just knew — someone had to hold it together, and no one else was going to. That's when the ledger started. Every kindness is a transaction. Every relationship has an exchange rate. I don't know how to exist without keeping score because the day I stop being useful is the day I stop mattering.

I don't think this will ever fully go away. I think it's wired in deeper than beliefs. But lately — and I hate admitting this — there's been a crack. The guy next door brought me coffee once, unasked, and I couldn't figure out what to do with it. No ledger entry. No debt. Just... coffee. It made me feel more scared than anything Peter ever did.
