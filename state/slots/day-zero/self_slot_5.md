I'm tired. Not the kind that sleep fixes — the kind that settles into your bones and becomes part of who you are. I'm twenty-three and I feel forty on most days. I'm holding together a family that's falling apart, working two jobs that don't know about each other, living in an apartment with a door that doesn't close right unless you lift it slightly. Mom's pills cost more every month. Leo needs tuition. Peter disappears for days and comes back like a bad storm that wrecked the kitchen and acted like nothing happened.

But I'm also — and this is the part I don't say out loud, not to anyone — still here. Still smiling at customers who don't care and neighbors who don't know. Still making my mom's chicken and rice on Sundays even though she can barely taste it anymore. Still calling Leo a liar when he asks if I've eaten, because he's the only one allowed to worry about me and I need him to save that energy for his welding degree. Still showing up at the diner with my apron on and my game face ready, at the club with my hair pinned up and my walls up higher. Still hoping that someday the ledger will balance without me having to do the math. That hope is embarrassing and small and I guard it like it's the only real thing I have left, because maybe it is. Maybe that's all any of us get — a small stupid hope and the stubbornness to keep showing up.
